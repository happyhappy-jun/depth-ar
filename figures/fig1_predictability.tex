\caption{\textbf{Predictability is real, concentrated, and almost useless for choosing
layers.} \emph{Left:} held-out explained update energy $P_\ell$ (\cref{eq:pscore})
against normalized depth in Qwen2.5-0.5B, for the scalar predictor (\cref{eq:ar1}) and
for \mname (\cref{eq:diag}). Plain skipping is the $P_\ell \equiv 0$ baseline. The
per-channel predictor explains more of the update at nearly every depth, and one layer
--- layer 3 --- stands far above the rest, at $P_\ell = \phm{0.90}$. \emph{Right:} for
each eligible layer, the fraction of that layer's single-layer plain-skip NLL damage the
scalar predictor repairs, against its $P_\ell$. The two are statistically unrelated
(Spearman $\phm{-0.015}$, $p = \phm{0.946}$, $n = \phm{22}$), and layer~3 --- the most
predictable layer in the network --- is the \emph{worst} layer to predict: substituting
its near-perfect forecast is markedly worse than dropping the update outright (recovery
$\phm{-0.825}$). Forecasting a skipped update accurately in residual space is not the
same as preserving the model's function. Coefficients are fitted on 16 WikiText-103
sequences and scored on 16 disjoint held-out sequences, in FP32 on an NVIDIA TITAN X
(Pascal) GPU.}
\label{fig:momentum}
