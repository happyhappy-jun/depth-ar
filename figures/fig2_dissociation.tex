\caption{\textbf{The same method, the same layers, two different verdicts.} Quality
against the fraction of blocks skipped, for Qwen2.5-0.5B (circles), Qwen2.5-1.5B
(squares) and Qwen2.5-7B (triangles), with layers chosen by measured residual damage ---
the deployable rule --- and every predictor skipping the identical blocks.
\emph{Left:} on WikiText-2 likelihood, \mname (solid) sits below plain skipping (dashed)
at every budget and all three scales, and below the scalar predictor (dotted); the inset
plots the difference against plain skipping, which is negative --- an improvement ---
throughout. \emph{Right:} on downstream accuracy the same curves at the same layer sets
are indistinguishable from plain skipping. Error bars and the shaded inset band are
$\pm 1$ binomial standard error; the accuracy differences straddle zero and \emph{no}
deployable difference reaches significance at the $95\%$ level, for any budget or scale
(\Cref{sec:app-noise}). The right panel is therefore an absence of effect, not a
regression --- and a $\pm 1$ SE band is conservative here, since a point inside it is a
fortiori inside a $95\%$ interval. The two panels are not a strong result and a weak
one: they are a repair measured in one currency and its absence in another. Recovering a
skipped layer's contribution to the loss is not recovering its contribution to the
model's behaviour. 0.5B in FP32 on an NVIDIA TITAN X (Pascal) GPU; 1.5B and 7B in BF16
on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090.}
