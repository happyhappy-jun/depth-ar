\paragraph{Setup.} We evaluate on Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B, and
Qwen2.5-\topscale \citep{qwen2025}. Coefficients are fitted in closed form
(\cref{eq:fit}) on unlabeled WikiText-103 text \citep{merity2017pointer} --- 16
sequences of length 512 for the 0.5B analysis, and \ph{32} for the larger models
--- with a disjoint held-out split of the same size used to score $P_\ell$ and to
measure recovery; no task data is used for calibration. Skipped blocks are
constrained to be non-adjacent, so that no skipped layer consumes a \emph{predicted}
update as the input to its own prediction. Every predictor is compared \emph{at an
identical skipped-layer set}, so differences isolate the update predictor rather
than the selection rule, and every selection rule is compared across identical
predictors. The 0.5B and 1.5B models run in FP32 on an NVIDIA TITAN X (Pascal)
GPU; the \topscale model runs in BF16 on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU. Neither
configuration uses FlashAttention. We report \emph{prefill} latency only,
CUDA-synchronized, at sequence lengths 512 and 2048 with \texttt{use\_cache=False};
we make no claim about end-to-end generation speed.

\paragraph{A single coefficient per layer is not enough.} \Cref{tab:selection}
reports the fraction of plain-skip NLL damage that each predictor recovers. The
scalar predictor of \cref{eq:ar1} is not a mildly weaker version of \mname; under
the cheap selection rule it is \emph{worse than plain skipping}, recovering
\phm{$-0.745$} of the damage at $k=2$ --- that is, it roughly doubles it. Granting
one coefficient per channel, and changing nothing else, turns that into
\phm{$+0.053$}. The gap persists under every selection rule and both budgets: at
$k=4$ with recovery-based selection, \mname repairs \phm{24.7}\% of the damage
against \phm{16.3}\% for the scalar. Copy Update is worse than plain skipping
everywhere, confirming that the benefit comes from \emph{fitting} the coefficients
and not from injecting a nonzero update.

\paragraph{Predictability does not imply recoverability.} The most striking
negative result is about \emph{selection}, not prediction, and it is visible in
\Cref{fig:momentum}. Across the 22 eligible layers of Qwen2.5-0.5B, held-out
predictability $P_\ell$ and the fraction of skipping damage a predictor actually
repairs are statistically unrelated (Spearman
\phm{$-0.015$}, $p = \phm{0.95}$). Layer 3 is the extreme case: a single scalar
explains \phm{90}\% of its update energy --- by far the most predictable layer in
the network --- and yet substituting that prediction is markedly worse than
dropping the update, nearly doubling the damage. Choosing layers by $P_\ell$ is
therefore not merely suboptimal, it is actively harmful (\Cref{tab:selection},
top block). This matters because $P_\ell$ is exactly the signal this method
appears to hand you for free, and it is the one a practitioner would reach for
first. Accurate reconstruction of the update in residual space is simply not the
same objective as preserving the model's function. The same blindness recurs one
level down, in hyperparameter selection: choosing the ridge coefficient of
\cref{eq:fit} by held-out $P_\ell$ selects a value that leaves development NLL at
\phm{11.01} against a dense \phm{2.73}. Because $P_\ell$ is energy-weighted,
low-energy channels barely register in it --- and it is precisely their
unregularized coefficients that wreck the model.

\paragraph{Depth is anti-correlated before it is autoregressive.} The momentum the
method assumes is not a general property of depth. Through the middle of
Qwen2.5-0.5B every fitted scalar coefficient is negative (layers 4--16, \phm{13} of
\phm{13}), and this is not an artifact of summarizing many channels with one number:
\phm{88}\% of the individual per-channel coefficients in that region are also
negative, with a median of \phm{$-0.19$}. Successive blocks there partially
\emph{undo} one another. Positive depth-wise momentum appears only in the last
quarter of the stack, and it is there --- together with the earliest layers --- that
the recoverable damage is concentrated.

\paragraph{The effect holds at scale.} \Cref{tab:main} carries the comparison to
Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-\topscale on downstream tasks, with layers chosen by
measured recovery. The ordering established on 0.5B is unchanged: Copy Update is
worse than plain skipping, the scalar predictor recovers a little, and \mname
recovers materially more, restoring \ph{29}\% of the average-accuracy drop at
$k=4$ on \topscale while leaving prefill latency within \phm{2}\% of plain
skipping's. The absolute recovery is modest, and we present it as such: replacing
the zero-update assumption is a real but bounded gain, not a way to skip layers for
free.

\paragraph{Why the per-channel predictor wins is not yet settled.} We can measure
that it does, and we can dissect one clear case of scalar failure: at layer 3 the
fitted scalar is \phm{$+1.10$} while the median channel coefficient is
\phm{$-0.20$} and \phm{76}\% of channels are negative, so the norm-weighted scalar
moves three quarters of the channels in the wrong direction. But this cannot be
the whole story, because \mname also outperforms the scalar at layers whose
channels agree in sign (\Cref{tab:selection}, middle block). We therefore report
the effect and leave its mechanism open; we regard identifying it as the most
interesting question this work raises. Full layer-wise profiles, the \mname(2)
comparison, and per-task results are in \Cref{sec:app-experiments}.
