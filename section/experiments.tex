\paragraph{Setup.} We evaluate Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-\topscale
\citep{qwen2025}. Coefficients are fitted in closed form (\cref{eq:fit}) on unlabeled
WikiText-103 \citep{merity2017pointer} and scored on a disjoint held-out split; no task
data is used for calibration. We report NLL and accuracy on HellaSwag
\citep{zellers2019hellaswag}, PIQA \citep{bisk2020piqa} and ARC-Easy
\citep{clark2018think} (\phm{100} examples per task on 0.5B, \phm{300} on 1.5B and
\topscale), and give accuracy deltas as absolute counts, since recovered-gap fractions
divide by very small denominators. Skipped blocks are non-adjacent, and within a budget
every predictor skips the \emph{identical} blocks, isolating the predictor from the
selection rule. Full protocol, hardware and precisions in \Cref{sec:app-protocol}.

\paragraph{A single coefficient per layer is not enough.} \Cref{tab:selection} reports the
fraction of plain-skip NLL damage each predictor recovers under three rules for choosing
which blocks to skip. The scalar predictor of \cref{eq:ar1} is not a mildly weaker \mname:
under the cheap rule it is \emph{worse} than plain skipping, recovering \phm{$-0.745$} of
the damage at $k=2$. One coefficient per channel, nothing else changed, turns that into
\phm{$+0.053$}. \mname recovers more damage than every alternative under \emph{every} rule
and both budgets, so it composes with whichever selection method a practitioner already
uses. Copy Update is worse than plain skipping everywhere, so the benefit comes from
\emph{fitting} the coefficients, not from injecting a nonzero update.

\paragraph{At a matched budget, likelihood recovery grows with scale.} \Cref{tab:main} carries the comparison
to downstream tasks. At the conservative, damage-based operating point, \mname recovers
\phm{4.6}\% of the plain-skip language-modelling damage on 0.5B, \phm{10.3}\% on 1.5B and
\phm{23.3}\% on 7B at $k=4$, the largest damage we repair anywhere in absolute terms
(\phm{0.4034} nats). The trend is budget-specific: at $k=2$ recovery is flat to mildly
declining with scale (\phm{8.4}\%, \phm{8.2}\%, \phm{7.3}\%), so we claim no general
scaling law. The scalar predictor recovers consistently less, and Copy Update is
worse than not predicting at all.

\paragraph{Under aggressive pruning, likelihood recovery becomes behaviour.} The skip
operator matters most where plain skipping breaks the model. Selecting layers to maximise
recovery on Qwen2.5-1.5B drives plain-skip loss to \phm{5.99} at $k=2$ against a dense
\phm{2.33}, collapsing downstream accuracy. At those layer sets \mname recovers \phm{70.0}\%
of the likelihood and answers \phm{132} and \phm{90} more questions correctly out of
\phm{900} than plain skipping ($z=\phm{6.45}$, $\phm{4.37}$), the only \phm{2} of \phm{37}
accuracy comparisons here to survive Bonferroni correction. The context matters: these are
gains over a badly damaged baseline, and the pruned models remain well below dense quality.
But they hold at a matched skipping fraction, which makes them the right comparison for
anyone who must compress hard.

\paragraph{Predictability does not imply recoverability.} Across the 22 eligible layers of
Qwen2.5-0.5B, held-out predictability $P_\ell$ and the damage a predictor actually repairs
are statistically unrelated (Spearman \phm{$-0.015$}, $p=\phm{0.95}$; \Cref{fig:momentum}),
and choosing layers by $P_\ell$ is not merely suboptimal but actively harmful
(\Cref{tab:selection}, top block). This matters because $P_\ell$ is exactly the signal the
method appears to hand you for free. Accurate reconstruction in residual space is simply
not the same objective as preserving function, and the same blindness recurs one level
down: selecting the ridge of \cref{eq:fit} by $P_\ell$ leaves development NLL at
\phm{11.01} against a dense \phm{2.73} (\Cref{sec:app-ridge}).

\paragraph{The forecast is cheap, and it buys frontier position.} On Qwen2.5-7B at four
skipped blocks, \mname adds \phm{0.64}\% \emph{prefill} latency over plain skipping at
sequence length 512, retaining a \phm{1.141}$\times$ prefill speedup over the dense model
(\phm{1.151}$\times$ at 2048) where plain skipping reaches \phm{1.148}$\times$
(\Cref{sec:app-latency}). The two methods sit at effectively the same point on the compute
axis, so all of \mname's advantage is quality at that point, and that advantage widens as
compression deepens: on 0.5B it recovers \phm{18.0}\% of the plain-skip damage at a quarter
of blocks skipped and \phm{42.6}\% at \phm{41.7}\% (\Cref{sec:app-pareto}). We measure
prefill latency at $k=4$ only and make no claim about end-to-end generation.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\label{sec:limitations}
At \emph{conservative} budgets, where plain skipping already preserves most of the model,
the likelihood \mname recovers does not register as a downstream accuracy gain. No accuracy
difference at the damage-based operating point is significant at any of the three scales, and
more tellingly, those differences are smaller than the run-to-run variation of the evaluation
itself: re-scoring an identical configuration under a different task batch shape moves up to
\phm{4} of \phm{900} answers (\Cref{sec:app-noise}). We claim no downstream improvement
there, and symmetrically no harm. Translating conservative-budget likelihood recovery into accuracy is
the natural next step. We also leave open \emph{why} the per-channel predictor succeeds
where the scalar fails (\Cref{sec:app-experiments}). One clue comes from a
\textbf{cross-token} variant, which also reads the previous \emph{token}'s update from the
preceding layer: it turns layer 2, the one pathology no ridge could suppress, from a recovery
of \phm{$-3.31$} into \phm{$+0.19$}, so that failure was missing information, not
mis-regularisation. On a single-layer 0.5B scan cross-token prediction raises median recovery
\phm{1.8}$\times$; it does not improve \mname at deployable layer sets, and we report it as
preliminary in \Cref{sec:app-crosstoken}.
