\paragraph{Setup.} We evaluate Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-\topscale
\citep{qwen2025}. Coefficients are fitted in closed form (\cref{eq:fit}) on unlabeled
WikiText-103 \citep{merity2017pointer} and scored on a disjoint held-out split; no task
data is used for calibration. We report NLL and accuracy on \phm{300} examples each of
HellaSwag \citep{zellers2019hellaswag}, PIQA \citep{bisk2020piqa} and ARC-Easy
\citep{clark2018think}. Skipped blocks are non-adjacent, so no skipped layer consumes a
\emph{predicted} update as its own input, and within a budget every predictor skips the
\emph{identical} blocks, isolating the predictor from the selection rule. The 0.5B runs
use FP32 on an NVIDIA TITAN X (Pascal) GPU; 1.5B and \topscale use BF16 on a single
NVIDIA RTX 3090. No configuration uses FlashAttention. Full protocol in
\Cref{sec:app-protocol}.

\paragraph{A single coefficient per layer is not enough.} \Cref{tab:selection}
reports the fraction of plain-skip NLL damage that each predictor recovers. The
scalar predictor of \cref{eq:ar1} is not a mildly weaker version of \mname; under
the cheap selection rule it is \emph{worse than plain skipping}, recovering
\phm{$-0.745$} of the damage at $k=2$ --- that is, it roughly doubles it. Granting
one coefficient per channel, and changing nothing else, turns that into
\phm{$+0.053$}. The gap persists under every selection rule and both budgets: at
$k=4$ with recovery-based selection, \mname repairs \phm{24.7}\% of the damage
against \phm{16.3}\% for the scalar. Copy Update is worse than plain skipping
everywhere, confirming that the benefit comes from \emph{fitting} the coefficients
and not from injecting a nonzero update.

\paragraph{Predictability does not imply recoverability.} The most striking
negative result is about \emph{selection}, not prediction, and it is visible in
\Cref{fig:momentum}. Across the 22 eligible layers of Qwen2.5-0.5B, held-out
predictability $P_\ell$ and the fraction of skipping damage a predictor actually
repairs are statistically unrelated (Spearman
\phm{$-0.015$}, $p = \phm{0.95}$). Layer 3 is the extreme case: a single scalar
explains \phm{90}\% of its update energy --- by far the most predictable layer in
the network --- and yet substituting that prediction is markedly worse than
dropping the update, nearly doubling the damage. Choosing layers by $P_\ell$ is
therefore not merely suboptimal, it is actively harmful (\Cref{tab:selection},
top block). This matters because $P_\ell$ is exactly the signal this method
appears to hand you for free, and it is the one a practitioner would reach for
first. Accurate reconstruction of the update in residual space is simply not the
same objective as preserving the model's function. The same blindness recurs one
level down, in hyperparameter selection: choosing the ridge coefficient of
\cref{eq:fit} by held-out $P_\ell$ selects a value that leaves development NLL at
\phm{11.01} against a dense \phm{2.73}. Because $P_\ell$ is energy-weighted,
low-energy channels barely register in it --- and it is precisely their
unregularized coefficients that wreck the model.

\paragraph{Likelihood recovers; behaviour does not.} \Cref{tab:main} carries the
comparison to downstream tasks. \mname improves language-modelling loss over plain
skipping at every scale and budget, but the accuracy gap it is supposed to close
does not close. At four skipped blocks with damage-based selection, likelihood
recovery is positive at both scales (\phm{4.6}\% on 0.5B, \phm{10.3}\% on 1.5B)
while accuracy recovery is \emph{negative} --- \phm{$-21.1$}\% and \phm{$-6.0$}\%
respectively. In absolute terms on 0.5B, \mname reaches \phm{0.510} average
accuracy where plain skipping reaches \phm{0.523}: predicting the missing update
leaves the model measurably \emph{worse} on task than deleting it, even as it makes
the model measurably better at predicting text. On 0.5B the sign is negative at
every budget we ran. The single budget at which accuracy does improve --- $k=2$ on
1.5B, \phm{18.5}\% --- is one cell out of the grid, and we report it as an
exception rather than a result. The depth-wise anti-correlation underlying all of
this is quantified in \Cref{sec:app-anticorr}.

\paragraph{Large recovery fractions can hide unacceptable models.} Selecting layers
to maximise recovery inflates the fraction while destroying the model. On 1.5B at
$k=4$ that rule yields \mname's largest likelihood recovery anywhere,
\phm{66.3}\%, at an absolute loss of \phm{4.01} against a dense \phm{2.33} --- a
model no one would deploy. Damage-based selection recovers a far smaller fraction,
\phm{10.3}\%, at an absolute loss of \phm{2.71}. The largest recovery fractions
arise precisely on the layer sets whose absolute quality is unacceptable, which is
the dissociation stated in the units practitioners actually optimize.

\paragraph{The forecast is cheap; that is not the problem.} On Qwen2.5-1.5B at four
skipped blocks, \mname adds \phm{0.23}\% \emph{prefill} latency over plain skipping at
sequence length 512 (\phm{0.18}\% at 2048), retaining a \phm{1.146}$\times$ prefill
speedup over the dense model where plain skipping reaches \phm{1.149}$\times$
(\Cref{sec:app-latency}). The per-channel forecast costs two tenths of one percent of
prefill latency and recovers a real fraction of the likelihood; what it does not buy is
downstream accuracy. We measure prefill only and make no claim about end-to-end
generation.

\paragraph{Why the per-channel predictor wins is not yet settled.} We can measure
that it does, and we can dissect one clear case of scalar failure: at layer 3 the
fitted scalar is \phm{$+1.10$} while the median channel coefficient is
\phm{$-0.20$} and \phm{76}\% of channels are negative, so the norm-weighted scalar
moves three quarters of the channels in the wrong direction. But this cannot be
the whole story, because \mname also outperforms the scalar at layers whose
channels agree in sign (\Cref{tab:selection}, middle block). We therefore report
the effect and leave its mechanism open; we regard identifying it as the most
interesting question this work raises. Full layer-wise profiles, the \mname(2)
comparison, and per-task results are in \Cref{sec:app-experiments}.
