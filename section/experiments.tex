\paragraph{Setup.} We evaluate Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-\topscale
\citep{qwen2025}. Coefficients are fitted in closed form (\cref{eq:fit}) on unlabeled
WikiText-103 \citep{merity2017pointer} and scored on a disjoint held-out split; no task
data is used for calibration. We report NLL and accuracy on HellaSwag
\citep{zellers2019hellaswag}, PIQA \citep{bisk2020piqa} and ARC-Easy
\citep{clark2018think} (\phm{100} examples per task on 0.5B, \phm{300} on 1.5B and
\topscale), and give accuracy deltas as absolute counts, since recovered-gap fractions
divide by very small denominators. Skipped blocks are non-adjacent, and within a budget
every predictor skips the \emph{identical} blocks, isolating the predictor from the
selection rule. Full protocol, hardware and precisions in \Cref{sec:app-protocol}.

\paragraph{A single coefficient per layer is not enough.} \Cref{tab:selection}
reports the fraction of plain-skip NLL damage each predictor recovers. The scalar
predictor of \cref{eq:ar1} is not a mildly weaker \mname; under the cheap selection
rule it is \emph{worse than plain skipping}, recovering \phm{$-0.745$} of the damage
at $k=2$ --- roughly doubling it. One coefficient per channel, and nothing else
changed, turns that into \phm{$+0.053$}, and the gap holds under every rule and
budget (\phm{24.7}\% against \phm{16.3}\% at $k=4$, recovery-selected). Copy Update
is worse than plain skipping everywhere, so the benefit comes from \emph{fitting} the
coefficients, not from injecting a nonzero update.

\paragraph{Predictability does not imply recoverability.} The most striking negative
result is about \emph{selection}, not prediction (\Cref{fig:momentum}). Across the 22
eligible layers of Qwen2.5-0.5B, held-out predictability $P_\ell$ and the damage a
predictor actually repairs are statistically unrelated (Spearman \phm{$-0.015$},
$p=\phm{0.95}$), and choosing layers by $P_\ell$ is not merely suboptimal but actively
harmful (\Cref{tab:selection}, top block). This matters because $P_\ell$ is exactly the
signal the method appears to hand you for free, and the one a practitioner reaches for
first: accurate reconstruction in residual space is simply not the same objective as
preserving function. The same blindness recurs one level down --- selecting the ridge of
\cref{eq:fit} by $P_\ell$ picks a value leaving development NLL at \phm{11.01} against a
dense \phm{2.73}, because an energy-weighted criterion cannot see the low-energy channels
whose unregularized coefficients wreck the model (\Cref{sec:app-ridge}).

\paragraph{Likelihood recovers; behaviour recovers only where the model is ruined.}
\Cref{fig:dissociation} carries the comparison to downstream tasks; the full
per-task numbers are in \Cref{tab:main} (\Cref{sec:app-experiments}).
\mname improves language-modelling loss over plain skipping at every scale and budget.
Its effect on accuracy splits cleanly by selection rule, and in neither case is it what
one would want. Choosing layers to \emph{maximise recovery} produces the only
statistically significant accuracy gains we measure anywhere: $+\phm{132}$ correct
answers of \phm{900} at $k=2$ on 1.5B ($z=\phm{6.45}$) and $+\phm{90}$ at $k=4$
($z=\phm{4.37}$). These are the only \phm{2} of \phm{16} comparisons to survive
Bonferroni correction, and they are earned on layer sets where plain skipping has already
driven language-modelling loss to \phm{5.99}--\phm{7.31} against a dense \phm{2.33}. The
gain is real, and it is a gain over a baseline nobody would ship.

At the damage-based operating point one would actually deploy, no accuracy difference is
significant at any of the three scales. The largest delta is $+\phm{22}$ answers of
\phm{900} on 7B ($z=\phm{1.18}$); across all three scales and every budget the deltas
run from \phm{$-6$} to $+\phm{22}$ answers, with several tasks \emph{exactly tied}. We
give counts rather than recovered-gap fractions throughout: the fractions divide by
denominators as small as \phm{27} answers, and five answers in a \phm{27}-answer gap is
not a result however it is typeset (\Cref{sec:app-noise}). We establish neither
improvement nor regression in behaviour at the deployable point, and claim neither.

\paragraph{The forecast is cheap; that is not the problem.} On Qwen2.5-7B at the same
four skipped blocks, \mname adds \phm{0.64}\% \emph{prefill} latency over plain skipping
at sequence length 512, and \phm{0.03}\% at 2048 --- the latter smaller than the
interquartile range of the measurement itself. It retains a \phm{1.141}$\times$ prefill
speedup over the dense model, where plain skipping reaches \phm{1.148}$\times$
(\Cref{sec:app-latency}). The per-channel forecast therefore costs well under one percent
of prefill latency and recovers a real fraction of the likelihood; what it does not buy,
at the deployable operating point, is any measurable downstream accuracy. The
dissociation is not a cost trade-off. We measure prefill only and make no claim about
end-to-end generation.

\paragraph{Why the per-channel predictor wins is not settled.} At layer 3 the fitted
scalar is \phm{$+1.10$} while the median channel coefficient is \phm{$-0.20$} and
\phm{76}\% of channels are negative, so the scalar moves three quarters of the channels
the wrong way. That cannot be the whole story, since \mname also beats the scalar where
channels agree in sign. We report the effect and leave the mechanism open; identifying it
is the most interesting question this work raises. Full profiles, the \mname(2)
comparison, the noise audit and per-task results are in \Cref{sec:app-experiments}.
