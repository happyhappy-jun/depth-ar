\paragraph{Setup.} We evaluate Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-\topscale
\citep{qwen2025}. Coefficients are fitted in closed form (\cref{eq:fit}) on unlabeled
WikiText-103 \citep{merity2017pointer} and scored on a disjoint held-out split; no task
data is used for calibration. We report NLL and accuracy on HellaSwag
\citep{zellers2019hellaswag}, PIQA \citep{bisk2020piqa} and ARC-Easy
\citep{clark2018think} --- \phm{100} examples per task on 0.5B and \phm{300} on 1.5B and
\topscale, which we hold fixed and report accuracy deltas as absolute counts against,
since the recovered-gap fractions divide by very small denominators. Skipped blocks are non-adjacent, so no skipped layer consumes a
\emph{predicted} update as its own input, and within a budget every predictor skips the
\emph{identical} blocks, isolating the predictor from the selection rule. The 0.5B runs
use FP32 on an NVIDIA TITAN X (Pascal) GPU; 1.5B and \topscale use BF16 on a single
NVIDIA RTX 3090. No configuration uses FlashAttention. Full protocol in
\Cref{sec:app-protocol}.

\paragraph{A single coefficient per layer is not enough.} \Cref{tab:selection}
reports the fraction of plain-skip NLL damage each predictor recovers. The scalar
predictor of \cref{eq:ar1} is not a mildly weaker \mname; under the cheap selection
rule it is \emph{worse than plain skipping}, recovering \phm{$-0.745$} of the damage
at $k=2$ --- roughly doubling it. One coefficient per channel, and nothing else
changed, turns that into \phm{$+0.053$}, and the gap holds under every rule and
budget (\phm{24.7}\% against \phm{16.3}\% at $k=4$, recovery-selected). Copy Update
is worse than plain skipping everywhere, so the benefit comes from \emph{fitting} the
coefficients, not from injecting a nonzero update.

\paragraph{Predictability does not imply recoverability.} The most striking
negative result is about \emph{selection}, not prediction, and it is visible in
\Cref{fig:momentum}. Across the 22 eligible layers of Qwen2.5-0.5B, held-out
predictability $P_\ell$ and the fraction of skipping damage a predictor actually
repairs are statistically unrelated (Spearman
\phm{$-0.015$}, $p = \phm{0.95}$). Layer 3 is the extreme case: a single scalar
explains \phm{90}\% of its update energy --- by far the most predictable layer in
the network --- and yet substituting that prediction is markedly worse than
dropping the update, nearly doubling the damage. Choosing layers by $P_\ell$ is
therefore not merely suboptimal, it is actively harmful (\Cref{tab:selection},
top block). This matters because $P_\ell$ is exactly the signal this method
appears to hand you for free, and it is the one a practitioner would reach for
first. Accurate reconstruction of the update in residual space is simply not the
same objective as preserving the model's function. The same blindness recurs one
level down, in hyperparameter selection: choosing the ridge coefficient of
\cref{eq:fit} by held-out $P_\ell$ selects a value that leaves development NLL at
\phm{11.01} against a dense \phm{2.73}. Because $P_\ell$ is energy-weighted,
low-energy channels barely register in it --- and it is precisely their
unregularized coefficients that wreck the model.

\paragraph{Likelihood recovers; behaviour recovers only where the model is ruined.}
\Cref{fig:dissociation} carries the comparison to downstream tasks; the full
per-task numbers are in \Cref{tab:main} (\Cref{sec:app-experiments}).
\mname improves language-modelling loss over plain skipping at every scale and budget.
Its effect on accuracy splits cleanly by selection rule. Choosing layers to
\emph{maximise recovery} produces a large and unambiguous accuracy gain --- \phm{132}
additional correct answers out of \phm{900} on 1.5B at $k=2$, and \phm{90} at $k=4$,
both far outside sampling noise --- but that rule selects layers whose removal has
already wrecked the model: plain-skip loss there is \phm{5.99} against a dense
\phm{2.33}. The gain is real, and it is a gain over a baseline nobody would ship.

At the damage-based operating point one would actually deploy, the effect vanishes.
Every accuracy delta we measure, at both scales and all budgets, lies within one
binomial standard error of zero: from \phm{$-6$} to $+\phm{5}$ answers, with several
tasks \emph{exactly tied}. We give counts rather than recovered-gap fractions because
the fraction divides by a Dense-minus-Plain-Skip denominator only \phm{27} answers wide
at $k=2$ on 1.5B, turning five answers --- one task tied --- into ``$18.5\%$ of the
accuracy gap recovered''; the negative fractions are no better founded
(\Cref{sec:app-noise}). We therefore establish neither improvement nor regression in
behaviour at the deployable point and claim neither.

So the two selection rules \emph{are} the dissociation. The rule that maximises measured
recovery earns \mname's largest likelihood recovery anywhere (\phm{66.3}\%) and its only
detectable accuracy gains --- on models whose absolute quality is unacceptable. The rule
that keeps quality acceptable earns a real but modest likelihood recovery
(\phm{10.3}\%) and no measurable accuracy effect at all. Recovery and functional repair
agree only once the model is already ruined enough for there to be something obvious to
repair. The depth-wise anti-correlation underlying this is quantified in
\Cref{sec:app-anticorr}.

\paragraph{The forecast is cheap; that is not the problem.} On Qwen2.5-1.5B at four
skipped blocks, \mname adds \phm{0.23}\% \emph{prefill} latency over plain skipping at
sequence length 512 (\phm{0.18}\% at 2048), retaining a \phm{1.146}$\times$ prefill
speedup over the dense model where plain skipping reaches \phm{1.149}$\times$
(\Cref{sec:app-latency}). The per-channel forecast costs two tenths of one percent of
prefill latency and recovers a real fraction of the likelihood; what it does not buy,
at the deployable operating point, is any measurable downstream accuracy. We measure
prefill only and make no claim about end-to-end generation.

\paragraph{Why the per-channel predictor wins is not settled.} We can dissect one
clear case of scalar failure: at layer 3 the fitted scalar is \phm{$+1.10$} while the
median channel coefficient is \phm{$-0.20$} and \phm{76}\% of channels are negative,
so the norm-weighted scalar moves three quarters of the channels the wrong way. That
cannot be the whole story, since \mname also beats the scalar where channels agree in
sign. We report the effect and leave the mechanism open; identifying it is the most
interesting question this work raises. Full profiles, the \mname(2) comparison, the
noise audit and per-task results are in \Cref{sec:app-experiments}.
