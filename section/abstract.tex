\begin{abstract}
When a Transformer block is skipped, its residual update is replaced by zero.
That operator is never argued for---it is simply what removing a layer has
come to mean---and it contradicts what we know about residual streams: depth
is a trajectory, and steps along a smooth trajectory should be predictable
from the steps before them. We test this literally. \textbf{Depth-AR}
(Depth-wise AutoRegressive update prediction) forecasts a skipped block's
update from the preceding updates, per channel and in closed form, fitted on
unlabeled text with no gradient training and adding $0.64\%$ to prefill
latency. Much of the missing update is indeed recoverable: Depth-AR restores
$4.6$--$23.3\%$ of the language-modelling damage skipping causes at light
budgets across Qwen2.5-0.5B, 1.5B and 7B, and up to $70.0\%$ under
recovery-maximising selection. But recovering the update is not the same as
recovering the model. Held-out predictability is statistically unrelated to
the damage a predictor actually repairs (Spearman $-0.015$, $p = 0.95$): the
single most predictable layer in the network is the one where substituting
its near-perfect forecast is \emph{worse} than dropping the update outright.
Downstream, significant gains appear only where skipping does severe
damage---at $28.6\%$ of blocks skipped on Qwen2.5-7B, Depth-AR answers $89$
more questions correctly out of $900$ than plain skipping ($z = 4.31$,
surviving Bonferroni correction)---and nowhere else. Repair pays off where
pruning bites, and predicting a layer well tells you nothing about whether
you should.
\end{abstract}
