\begin{abstract}
Layer skipping makes Transformer inference cheaper by deleting entire residual blocks,
but the operator it uses to do so is crude: it assumes the missing residual update is
exactly zero. That update is in fact partly predictable from the updates that precede it.
We introduce \textbf{\mname (Depth-wise AutoRegressive update prediction)}, which replaces
a skipped block with a closed-form autoregressive forecast of its residual update, fitted
per channel on unlabeled text with no gradient training. \mname adds only \phm{0.64}\% to
prefill latency, yet recovers a substantial share of the language-modelling loss that
skipping destroys: \phm{4.6}\% to \phm{23.3}\% of the damage at conservative pruning
budgets across Qwen2.5-0.5B, 1.5B and 7B, and up to \phm{70.0}\% when layers are chosen to
maximise recovery. At four skipped blocks the recovery grows with scale, reaching
\phm{23.3}\% on 7B. Under aggressive pruning, where plain skipping collapses the model,
the recovered likelihood also translates into behaviour: at a matched skipping fraction
\mname answers \phm{132} more questions correctly out of \phm{900} than plain skipping
does on Qwen2.5-1.5B, a gain that survives Bonferroni correction across all \phm{16}
comparisons we run, though the pruned models remain well below dense quality. Getting this to work required discarding the obvious version
of the idea. A single global coefficient per layer fails, and at some layers is worse than
dropping the update outright; only a $d$-dimensional coefficient vector succeeds. At
conservative budgets the recovered likelihood does not yet show up in downstream accuracy,
and closing that gap is the natural next step.
\end{abstract}
