\begin{abstract}
Layer skipping makes Transformer inference cheaper by deleting entire residual blocks, but the
operator it uses assumes the missing residual update is exactly zero, and it is not: much of that
update is predictable from the updates preceding it. We introduce \textbf{\mname (Depth-wise
AutoRegressive update prediction)}, which replaces a skipped block with a closed-form,
per-channel autoregressive forecast of its update, fitted on unlabeled text with no gradient
training and adding $0.64\%$ to prefill latency. It recovers $4.6\%$ to $23.3\%$ of the
language-modelling damage skipping causes at light budgets across Qwen2.5-0.5B, 1.5B and 7B, and
up to $70.0\%$ under severe damage. Downstream the gains are real but conditional: at $28.6\%$ of
blocks skipped on Qwen2.5-7B, under the deployable selection rule, \mname answers 89 more
questions correctly out of 900 than plain skipping ($z=4.31$), one of four results surviving
Bonferroni correction over all 63 comparisons. Every one occurs where plain skipping costs at
least $27.7$ accuracy points; below that, the effects fall under the evaluation's own measured
reproducibility floor. Severe damage is necessary for repair to pay off downstream, and not
sufficient: repair pays off where pruning bites.
\end{abstract}
