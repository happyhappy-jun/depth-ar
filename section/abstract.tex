\begin{abstract}
Layer skipping makes Transformer inference cheaper by deleting entire residual
blocks, but the operator it uses to do so is crude: it assumes the missing
residual update is exactly zero. We ask whether that update is instead
\emph{predictable} from computation the model has already performed. We introduce
\textbf{\mname (Depth-wise AutoRegressive update prediction)}, which replaces a
skipped block with a closed-form autoregressive forecast of its residual update,
fitted per channel on unlabeled text with no gradient training, at $O(Td)$
inference cost. Getting this right required discarding the obvious version of the
idea. A single global coefficient per layer --- the natural first instantiation ---
fails, and at some layers is \emph{worse than dropping the update outright}; only
a $d$-dimensional coefficient vector, one coefficient per channel, recovers the
lost computation. Two further findings sharpen the picture. First, depth is not
uniformly autoregressive: through the middle of Qwen2.5-0.5B successive blocks
partially \emph{undo} one another, with \phm{88}\% of channels carrying a negative
coefficient. Second, and more consequentially for practice, \emph{predictability
does not imply recoverability} --- how well a layer's update can be forecast is
statistically unrelated to how much damage forecasting it repairs (Spearman
\phm{$-0.015$}, $p = \phm{0.95}$), so the cheap selection signal that this method
seems to hand you is the wrong one to use. Selecting layers by measured recovery
instead, \mname repairs up to \phm{24.7}\% of the language-modelling damage that
plain skipping causes at matched depth, against \phm{16.3}\% for the scalar
predictor. Plain skipping discards computation the model has already forecast ---
but forecasting it accurately is not the same as getting it back.
\end{abstract}
