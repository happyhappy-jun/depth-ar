\begin{abstract}
Layer skipping makes Transformer inference cheaper by deleting entire residual blocks, but the
operator it uses to do so assumes the missing residual update is exactly zero, and it is not:
much of that update is predictable from the updates that precede it. Predicting it instead of
discarding it recovers up to 70\% of the language-modelling damage skipping causes, and, where
skipping is destructive, a large and statistically significant share of the downstream accuracy
it costs, for 0.64\% added prefill latency. We introduce \textbf{\mname (Depth-wise
AutoRegressive update prediction)}, which replaces a skipped block with a closed-form
autoregressive forecast of its residual update, fitted per channel on unlabeled text with no
gradient training. Across Qwen2.5 (0.5B to 7B), the benefit of repair scales with the damage skipping inflicts. At
light budgets, where plain skipping barely hurts, the accuracy effects sit below the
evaluation's own measured reproducibility floor. Where the damage is severe, the gains are large
and survive Bonferroni correction across all 63 comparisons we run: at 28.6\% of blocks skipped
on Qwen2.5-7B, under the deployable damage-based selection rule, \mname answers 89 more
questions correctly out of 900 than plain skipping ($z=4.31$) while recovering 38.1\% of its
language-modelling damage. Prediction pays off in proportion to what skipping destroys, an
ordering a first out-of-family test on Qwen3 bounds rather than confirms. Getting this to work
required discarding the obvious version of the idea: a single global coefficient per layer
fails, and at some layers is worse than dropping the update outright.
\end{abstract}
