\begin{abstract}
Layer skipping makes Transformer inference cheaper by deleting entire residual blocks, but
the operator it uses to do so is crude: it assumes the missing residual update is exactly
zero. That update is in fact partly predictable from the updates that precede it. We
introduce \textbf{\mname (Depth-wise AutoRegressive update prediction)}, which replaces a
skipped block with a closed-form autoregressive forecast of its residual update, fitted per
channel on unlabeled text with no gradient training. \mname adds only 0.64\% to prefill
latency, yet recovers a substantial share of the language-modelling loss that skipping
destroys: 4.6\% to 23.3\% of the damage at light pruning budgets across
Qwen2.5-0.5B, 1.5B and 7B, and up to 70.0\% when layers are chosen to maximise
recovery. Across the Qwen2.5 family, its benefit to downstream behaviour scales with the
damage that skipping inflicts.
At light budgets, where plain skipping barely hurts, the accuracy effects sit below the
evaluation's own reproducibility floor. Where the damage is severe, the gains are large and
survive Bonferroni correction across all 63 comparisons we run: at 28.6\% of
blocks skipped on Qwen2.5-7B, under the deployable damage-based selection rule, \mname
answers 89 more questions correctly out of 900 than plain skipping
($z=4.31$) while recovering 38.1\% of its language-modelling damage. Prediction
pays off in proportion to what skipping destroys, an ordering that a first out-of-family
test on Qwen3 bounds rather than confirms. Getting this to work required discarding
the obvious version of the idea: a single global coefficient per layer fails, and at some
layers is worse than dropping the update outright; only a $d$-dimensional coefficient vector
succeeds.
\end{abstract}
