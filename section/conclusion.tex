\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Plain skipping treats a missing residual update as zero, and that is wasteful: the update
is partly predictable, and \mname recovers a real fraction of the likelihood it destroys
in closed form at $O(Td)$ cost --- but only per channel, since a single coefficient is at
some layers worse than dropping the update outright. The recovery stops at the loss.
Downstream accuracy follows it only on layer sets plain skipping has already ruined; at
the operating point one would deploy, the accuracy effect is undetectable at our sample
sizes, in either direction. Nor is that a cost trade-off: \mname adds \phm{0.64}\% prefill
latency on 7B at sequence length 512. Predictability, energy-weighted fit criteria and
language-modelling loss recovery are all cheaper proxies for functional preservation, and
all three overestimate it. We think that is the more useful finding, and the one a method
built on any of those proxies has to answer.
